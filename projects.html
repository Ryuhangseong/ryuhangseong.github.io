CODENAME: SISFIRE

This level is designed to create an exciting gaming experience and test players' strategic planning and teamwork abilities. Players will face multiple challenges within 15 minutes. Only through wisdom and courage can they successfully complete the battle.

PRE-PRODUCTION

During the pre-production phase, I delved into the core concept of the game and established the goals that the player needs to defeat the power armor-equipped boss "Sisfire" and escape the courtyard. This stage involves preliminary planning of game goals, enemy mechanics, player emotions, and environment design.

BEAT SHEET


Phase 1

Courtyard Exploration

Players are born in a back alley and walk through the back alley into the courtyard area. Players can explore the entire courtyard and find escape exits.

Phase 2

Boss Appears

After all players enter the courtyard, the Boss appears from the gate as VO plays, and the battle is about to begin. The boss wears armor that is almost immune to electricity, and the electromagnetic field causes damage to players.

Phase 3

Guide the Boss to the Electric Field

Players need to attract the hatred of the Boss and lead the Boss to the electric field. At the same time, the player who controls the switch triggers the switch to overload the voltage, causing the Boss's current armor and electromagnetic field to fail.

Phase 4

Concentrated Firepower

The player concentrates firepower to cause damage to the Boss, and repeats this cycle until the Boss is killed.

Phase 5

Boss phase switching

Every time the Boss switches phases, he will release a full-field AOE attack, and players need to dodge and reduce the damage.

Phase 6

Level Victory

After killing the Boss, the player picks up the dropped permission card, goes to the gate and uses the permission card to leave the courtyard and win the level.

Front Vector

When I was designing the cover-to-cover combat space, I was thinking of enemy presentation, goals, settings, narrative, and mechanics. I planned out the front vector first, then I designed cover orientation, cover interaction, enemy/player position, entrance, and exits based on the space vectors.

Combat Module

To reproduce more combat space, I analyzed the combat space in the Division series and listed some space modules to determine the combat space, in this way, I could quickly assemble the battlefield based on different vectors. For example, if there is a flank vector and I want the player to utilize the flank, I will insert a channel or a hallway.

Hallway: transitory space, encouraging movement, come and go quickly.
Channel: possibility for loops.
Sides: promote movements away from its center.
Middle map: compromised shelter, clear boundary, break sightline.
Hideout: lacks combat opportunity, shallow space, player forced to flee if enemy entered.
Beside the switch: dead end, enough opportunity, commit to the space, stand on the ground.

NAVIGATE


CAN YOU TELL ME WHERE WE ARE?

One of the key elements of a Division multiplayer map is the circulation of players in the space, as it completely modifies how they approach the environment and the gameplay.

Through the various iterations, I have built the map with a main spiraling path around the central courtyard, taking the players in a natural and constant movement loop that sparks genuine and conscious action. It also allows players to more easily memorize the environment, having a clear understanding of each location and making sure they can always decide the best course of action for what they need.

To help differentiate the environments and allow players to make informed decisions when navigating the loop, entrances, doors, and transitional spaces create chokepoints that enhance the attention and force players to work together to confidently navigate the environment.

CLOSING THOUGHTS


I learned a lot in this project in which I got to practice many techniques I learned at the same time. I also get a chance to learn more blueprint scripting skills which gives me more freedom when designing a level. Something I keep doing is playtesting. I was able to playtest in an early stage with others because I was developing new gameplay mechanisms at the same time. It slowed down my iteration process and I had to rework many parts every time I finished a new mechanism. It also taught me how dangerous to design a level when the core gameplay isn't settled yet. Furthermore, it affects me later on that it is barely possible to rework some parts. If I had more time to work, I would polish the combat logic and make the 3C more smooth to use. These are the fundamental segments I need to consider before I even start designing the level. I have many thoughts in mind, but I was restricted by the 3C I have.
